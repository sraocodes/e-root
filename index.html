E-root Overview

Introduction

The E-root project, funded by FNRS Belgium, was a pioneering endeavor from 2016 to 2020 in the field of plant-root-soil interactions, spearheaded by three distinguished experts: Sarah Gare, Mathieu Javaux, and Frederic Nguyen. Under their guidance, two dedicated Ph.D. students, Sathyanarayan Rao and Solomon Ehosioke, embarked on an ambitious journey to unlock the secrets of the electrical signatures of plant roots in soil.

Electrical Properties of roots

In simple terms, the electrical properties of roots are essential because they help us understand how plants interact with the soil and how they take up water and nutrients. This knowledge is crucial for sustainable agriculture and food production. Traditional methods of studying soil and roots have limitations in capturing the dynamic and complex patterns in the root zone. Geophysical methods, like electrical measurements, offer a noninvasive way to study the root system's distribution and its impact on soil conditions. These methods can help farmers improve irrigation efficiency, map soil properties for precision agriculture, and study fundamental interactions between plants and the environment.

E-root Achievements

We're thrilled to share some groundbreaking achievements from the E-Root project, where we've been delving deep into the fascinating world of soil-root electrical interaction. Our efforts have led to some exciting milestones that promise to revolutionize our understanding of plant roots and their electric signatures.

The first major breakthrough came in the form of a sophisticated soil-root finite element model. This powerful tool has the ability to simulate both electric and water flow equations, thanks to the ingenious combination of state-of-the-art models like R-SWMS, C-Plantbox, GMSH, EIDORS, and others. This coupled model enables us to explore the electrical signatures of various root architectures and conduct virtual geo-electric measurements. The applications of this model extend beyond merely understanding root electrical properties; it can also optimize injection schemes for geo-electric methods, such as Electrical Resistivity Tomography on cropped soils. This opens up a whole new realm of possibilities for precision agriculture and environmental monitoring. Please see the publication (Rao et al. Vadose Zone Journal, 2019)

But that's not all: we didn't stop there! The second achievement of the E-Root project involves pioneering experiments at the single root segment scale. These experiments aim to quantify the induced polarization signatures of plant roots, an essential aspect of developing non-invasive electrical monitoring techniques. Understanding root processes like nutrient or water uptake and root growth without disturbing the natural environment is crucial, and our novel experiments have paved the way for significant advancements in this area.(Ehosioke et al. Vadose Zone Journal, 2023)

Throughout the project, our team has honed its expertise in modeling soil-root systems at different scales – from individual plants to vast fields. This includes mastering electric and water processes and setting up experiments to characterize plant electrical properties. The knowledge gained is not only vital for the success of our project but also holds immense promise for future studies in the field of plant-root interactions.

Sathyanarayan Rao's Thesis

Introduction

Sathyanarayan Rao's worked on an exciting Ph.D. project as part of the E-root initiative. The goal was to explore how plant roots impact the electrical properties of soil. We used Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT), a valuable tool to study soil water movement in the root zone. However, traditional ERT results didn't consider the influence of roots on the soil's electrical conductivity. In this project, We developed a special model to understand the electrical processes happening in the soil-plant system when exposed to an external electric field. At a microscopic level, We created a new model that considered how soil and roots' polarization affected the electrical behavior. We also studied how different root characteristics influenced these properties. Next, We worked on electrical models for individual root segments and integrated them into complete, growing root architectures. This helped predict and understand the effective electrical properties of the soil-root system. To make these models more accurate, they combined them with existing root growth and soil water models. This allowed us to study the relationship between electrical properties and plant growth. Overall, Sathyanarayan's research contributed to a better understanding of how plants and soil interact electrically, and it may lead to improved agricultural monitoring practices in the future.

Figure showing current lines within root zone

The Central Objective

The central objective of this thesis was to develop a numerical model capable of capturing both direct and indirect electrical impacts of plant roots in geoelectric measurements, specifically electrical resistivity tomography (ERT). In this study, a combination of surface and depth electrodes with high spatial resolution was used to map 2-D changes of bulk soil electrical conductivity (EC) in an agronomic trial with different herbaceous species. The impact of root segments was incorporated in the forward electrical model using the finite element scheme.

Figure showing a rhizotron finite element model used in the thesis

Methodology and Findings

The thesis successfully achieved one of the closest computer model representations of a rooted soil in understanding electrical signatures of root systems in soils. The findings from Sathyanarayan's thesis opened up new avenues for understanding root-soil electrical interactions,demonstrating how electrical measurements could offer valuable insights into root architecture and water uptake patterns. This newfound knowledge has practical applications in crop phenotyping using geoelectric methods such as Electrical Resistivity tomography and GPR and remote sensing,and beyond, where optimizing irrigation strategies, monitoring root health, and assessing soil properties become more informed and effective. Solomon Ehosioke's Thesis

Introduction

Solomon Ehosioke's Ph.D. thesis within the E-root project embarks on a captivating journey into the realm of root zone investigation using low-frequency electrical techniques. Root systems play a crucial role in the growth and development of plants, and understanding their intricate processes has been a subject of unwavering curiosity for Solomon. His research delves into the electrical conduction and polarization mechanisms within the soil-root continuum, seeking to shed light on the fascinating world of roots.

Set-Up and Findings

In his thesis, Solomon rigorously evaluates various electrical methods for root investigation, providing insights into their potential and limitations. From current injection in the plant stem to multi-electrode measurements, he explores the nuances of these techniques to better understand their applicability in root zone studies. Among these methods, frequency-dependent impedance measurements stood out as a promising avenue, enabling a spectroscopic approach that captures both surface and volumetric properties.

A key takeaway from Solomon's research is the recognition of the importance of understanding small-scale polarization and conduction mechanisms. This understanding is essential to accurately interpret electrical signatures at larger field scales. Computer models often needs validation through meticulous small-scale electrical measurements that this thesis achieved through meticulous experiments on root systems, aiming to enhance the precision and accuracy of multi-electrode tomographic techniques for root zone investigation. We also found that root anatomy is a controlling factor for the electrical signature. Resistivity magnitude correlated with cell wall and void density of plant roots, while phase peak correlated with root area and were plant species dependent.
